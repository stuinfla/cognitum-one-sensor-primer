01 — ORIENTATION

What is a Cognitum One Seed?

Before sensors, understand the thing you just unboxed. The Seed is not a sensor itself — it's a brain.

The Cognitum One Seed is a tiny, self-contained AI computer — it runs on-device intelligence with no cloud required. It senses the space around it, remembers what it learns as mathematical "fingerprints," and can recognise patterns and people passively — just by watching, never by asking you to scan anything.

Out of the box it has a few simple built-in senses (motion, vibration, temperature/humidity/pressure, and an analog input or two). Those are enough to learn the rhythm of a room. But the moment you want to sense finer things — exactly where a body is, a heartbeat through the air, the shape of a person with no camera — you connect the sensors in this guide. Some wire straight into the Seed; the richer ones run on a small companion computer that talks to it (that's the very next section).

02 — HOW IT SENSES

How sensors actually reach the Seed

This is the part most guides skip — and the thing you most need to know. There are two ways a sensor's data gets to your Seed, and picking the right one saves you days.

Path A — wire it straight into the Seed

The Seed reads simple sensors bolted to its own pins, 10 times a second, and folds them into its memory "fingerprint." This works on the Seed today, with no extra computer.

  • Digital pins (GPIO): motion (PIR), vibration, a door/reed switch — simple on/off signals
  • Analog sensors go through an ADS1115 analog-to-digital converter on the I²C bus (a ~$5 chip) — flex sensor, light, a load-cell amp, a potentiometer
  • Declare each one in the Seed's sensor-config.json; it samples at 10 Hz

Path B — run it on a companion computer

Rich sensors — mmWave radar, vital-signs radar, WiFi CSI, fast IMUs — produce far more data than the Seed's pins can take. They run on a small companion computer (e.g. a Raspberry Pi 5) that does the heavy sensing and talks to the Seed over the network (HTTP / MCP).

  • Radar & IMU stream over USB/serial or WiFi to the host
  • WiFi-CSI nodes (ESP32-S3/C5) stream to the host over WiFi
  • The host fuses everything and shares results with the Seed

⚠ The honest truth about WiFi CSI & the Seed

A Seed's own built-in WiFi chip cannot produce CSI — that's why WiFi-sensing needs separate ESP32-S3 / C5 nodes (which can) streaming to a companion host. If you read "WiFi DensePose, no cameras" and expected your Seed to do it alone, this is why it doesn't: the Seed's WiFi radio doesn't expose the raw data. Plan for ESP32 nodes + a host.

Your mileage may vary: the exact pins, ports and config field names in this guide come from one reference build. Always confirm against your own device's sensor-config.json and firmware before wiring.

03 — YOUR FIRST 15 MINUTES

From box to first signal

Pick the path that matches what you bought. Each ends in a concrete "you should now see this" moment — your first win.

Path A · a sensor wired to the Seed

  1. Power the Seed and give it a minute to boot.
  2. Wire one simple sensor — a PIR motion module: signal → a GPIO pin, VCC → 3V3, GND → GND. (Analog sensor instead? Run it through an ADS1115 on the I²C pins.)
  3. Add the sensor to the Seed's sensor-config.json (name + pin/driver) and let the 10 Hz loop pick it up.
  4. Win: wave your hand — the Seed registers the motion and its activity "fingerprint" shifts. You're now feeding the brain.

Path B · a sensor + a companion host

  1. Power the companion host and open its sensor dashboard.
  2. Power your sensor with a real USB-C data cable (not power-only).
  3. It auto-joins the WiFi it was flashed for — 2.4 GHz for S3/C6, 5 GHz for C5.
  4. Win: within ~30 s the sensor's address appears on the host; CSI packets land ~10×/sec, or an MR60 shows heart & breathing after a ~60 s warm-up.

The one fact that bites everyone first

Notice the 2.4 GHz vs. 5 GHz column. The ESP32-S3 and the original ESP32 are 2.4 GHz only — by silicon. They physically cannot join a 5 GHz-only WiFi network, no matter what you configure. Only the ESP32-C5 speaks 5 GHz. If a board "won't connect" to your fast network, this is almost always why — it's not broken, it's the wrong band. Plan accordingly: a 2.4 GHz network for S3/C6 sensors, a 5 GHz one for C5 sensors.

The six sensor classes — and what each one gives you

An ESP32 is just the brainstem of a sensor. What matters is the physics module bolted to it. Here is every sensor class in this ecosystem, in beginner terms.

Tells you where a person is on a 2D floor plan — their X/Y position and how fast they're moving — by bouncing 24 GHz radio off them. No camera, works in total darkness, sees through clothing.

WiFi CSI · presence & shape

ESP32 CSI nodes

ESP32-S3 (2.4 GHz) & ESP32-C5 (5 GHz)

The cleverest trick here: an ordinary WiFi chip reads how radio waves scatter off your body (Channel State Information). With several nodes you get rough body shape, presence, and breathing — through walls, with no dedicated radar at all.

Gotcha → "Subcarriers" are how many frequency-slices of detail you get — more is sharper. Use a mesh of nodes; one node alone can't tell left from right.
IMU · vibration & motion

M5StickC PLUS2

A tiny battery-powered gadget you attach to a moving thing to measure its acceleration and tilt — the only sensor here that streams raw motion live over WiFi. Used to measure how vibration travels through a body or a surface.

BLE wearable · ground truth

Polar H10

Bluetooth LE chest strap

A medical-grade chest strap for heart rate and beat-to-beat (R-R) timing. You don't use it day-to-day — it's the ruler you measure your contactless sensors against, to prove the radar HR is accurate.

Also in the family but superseded: the MPU-6050 (GY-521) — a bare I²C accelerometer, the predecessor to the M5's MPU6886. It works, but the M5StickC replaces it for most uses because it adds WiFi streaming and a battery.

06 — DECISION GUIDE

Radar vs. CSI vs. vitals vs. IMU — which do I use?

They overlap, but each is best at one job. Here's the cheat sheet.

TechnologyFrequencyBest atSpatial detailThrough walls?Wearable-free?

LD6004 radar60 GHz3D position incl. height±5 cm XY · ±15 cm ZNoyes

ESP32 WiFi CSI2.4 / 5 GHzPresence & rough shape, anywhereCoarse (~30 cm)yesyes

Reach for RADAR (LD2450 / LD6004) when…

  • you need to know where a body is, precisely
  • you want speed / sway / depth-of-movement
  • lighting is bad or privacy rules out cameras
  • you need heart rate (radar position can't do that)

Reach for WiFi CSI when…

  • you want coverage through walls / whole-room
  • you can place several nodes for triangulation
  • rough presence & shape is enough
  • you need centimetre-accurate position (too coarse)

Reach for mmWave VITALS (MR60) when…

  • you want heart rate & breathing with nothing worn
  • the subject is roughly still, facing the sensor
  • they're moving around the room (use radar instead)

Reach for an IMU (M5 / MPU) when…

  • you can attach a sensor to the thing you're measuring
  • you need fast, raw vibration or tilt data
  • contactless is a requirement (IMUs must be on-body)

The real power is in combining them

No single sensor is the whole picture. A radar says where, CSI says roughly what shape, the vitals radar says how hard the heart is working, the IMU says how the body is vibrating. The Seed's job is to fuse these streams into one understanding — and each sensor you add makes that understanding sharper and harder to fool. Start with one, add more as you grow.

Each USB radar draws ~100–200 mA; the 3.3 V rail is shared and small. If you add several USB sensors, use a powered USB hub. For more than one GPIO sensor, add an external 3.3 V supply. And give a Raspberry Pi 5 its proper 27 W (5 V / 5 A) supply — underpowering causes mystery dropouts that look like sensor failures but aren't.

⚠ The five gotchas that waste an afternoon

  • Power-only USB ports. Some boards (the MR60 XIAO) have a charge-only port and a separate data port. Flashing fails silently on the wrong one.
  • 5 GHz vs 2.4 GHz. An S3 or C6 will never see a 5 GHz network. Put it on 2.4 GHz.
  • Wrong baud rate. The LD2450 wants 256000, not the usual 115200. Wrong = all zeros.
  • TX/RX not crossed. Serial wires cross over. Swap them if you get nothing.
  • Cheap "charge" cables. Many USB-C cables carry power but no data. Buy cables labelled data.

How long will it last? Quick runtime math

A CSI/streaming ESP32 keeps its WiFi radio on, so budget roughly 120–160 mA average (peaks higher). Runtime ≈ battery mAh ÷ average mA:

  • 1000 mAh LiPo → ~6–8 hours
  • 2000 mAh LiPo → ~12–16 hours (a full day of use)
  • 3500 mAh 18650 → ~22–28 hours

Want days instead of hours? Have the firmware deep-sleep between readings (drops to microamps) — but note continuous CSI sensing can't sleep, so for an always-on mesh, plan to recharge daily or wire those nodes to USB wall power where outlets allow.

⚠ Battery safety — three rules

LiPo and Li-ion cells store a lot of energy. (1) Never charge them unattended on flammable surfaces. (2) Never puncture, crush, or fold a pouch — if it swells, retire it. (3) Always use a proper charger (TP4056 or an onboard charger), never raw voltage. For a room full of scattered nodes, lean toward boards with built-in protection circuits.

09 — GO FURTHER

Flash a node, then grow your system

Once your first sensor works, here's how to program a fresh WiFi-CSI node, where the data goes, what to do when something breaks, and the order to add sensors as you scale.

Flashing an ESP32 CSI node — the short version

  1. Install the toolchain once: ESP-IDF (Espressif's SDK), which brings esptool, the flasher.
  2. Open the firmware config (idf.py menuconfig) and set four things that live on the chip after flashing: a node ID, the WiFi SSID + password to join, the host IP to send to, and the UDP port (5005 for CSI).
  3. Build & flash: idf.py set-target esp32s3 (or esp32c5) → idf.py build → idf.py -p /dev/<your-port> flash monitor.
  4. Verify: on the host, run tcpdump -ni any udp port 5005 — you should see ~10 packets/second arriving from the new node.

Remember the data-vs-power port: flash over a port that carries data. On the MR60 XIAO that means opening the case for the inner USB-C.

"Why isn't it working?" — run this checklist

  • No power / wrong cable. Confirm the LED, then swap to a known data cable.
  • Wrong WiFi band. An S3/C6 can't see 5 GHz; a C5 may be looking for the 5 GHz network. Match the band.
  • Port blocked. Open UDP 5005 / 5007 on the host's firewall.
  • Wrong baud. The LD2450 wants 256000, not 115200.
  • TX/RX not crossed on GPIO sensors. Swap the two data wires.
  • IP drifted. The sensor got a new DHCP address — reserve its MAC in your router, or look it up fresh.
  • Still warming up. Vital-signs radar reads null for the first ~60 s. Wait.

11 — FAQ

Common questions

The things a brand-new owner usually asks next.

Can I start with just the Seed?
Yes. Wire a simple sensor — motion, vibration, or an analog sensor via an ADS1115 — straight into the Seed. That's Path A and it works today. Radar, vital-signs and WiFi-CSI sensors need a companion host (Path B).
What's the cheapest way to begin?
For position, a single LD2450 (~$8) + a USB-UART bridge. For heart & breathing, one MR60BHA2. For wired-to-the-Seed sensing, a PIR module and an ADS1115 are only a few dollars.
Does it send my data to the cloud?
The Seed runs its intelligence on-device — no cloud required — and these sensors are contactless (no camera). What you do with a companion host is your choice.
Can the Seed "see through walls" by itself?
No. WiFi sensing (CSI) needs separate ESP32-S3 / C5 nodes streaming to a companion host — the Seed's own WiFi chip can't produce CSI.
Which sensor should I buy first?
Match it to your goal: precise position → LD2450; heart & breathing → MR60BHA2; whole-room / through-wall presence → ESP32 CSI nodes; vibration of an object → an IMU (M5StickC).
How many sensors do I need?
Start with one. A useful WiFi-CSI mesh wants 3–4+ nodes; position + vitals wants a radar plus an MR60. Follow the growth path above and add as you go.
12 — PLAIN ENGLISH

Glossary: the jargon, decoded

Every term in this guide, explained like you've never seen it before.

mmWave
"Millimetre-wave" radio (24–60 GHz). Very short wavelengths bounce cleanly off bodies, giving fine motion and position detail.
FMCW radar
The technique radar sensors use: sweep a frequency, listen for the echo, and read distance & speed from the shift.
Doppler
The frequency change when something moves toward/away — how mmWave vitals sensors detect a chest rising or a heart beating.
CSI
Channel State Information — the fine detail of how a WiFi signal was distorted on its way to the receiver. Bodies distort it, so you can "see" with WiFi.
Subcarrier
WiFi splits its channel into dozens of parallel frequency lanes. Each is a subcarrier. More subcarriers = more detail in a CSI reading.
HT40
A "wide" 40 MHz WiFi channel. Wider channel = more subcarriers = richer CSI. (HT20 is the narrower, lower-detail mode.)
IMU
Inertial Measurement Unit — an accelerometer (+gyro) chip that senses motion, tilt and vibration of whatever it's attached to.
UART / serial
A simple two-wire way (transmit + receive) for a sensor to talk to a computer. Needs a USB-UART adapter or GPIO pins.
GPIO
The row of pins on a Raspberry Pi you connect wires to — for power, ground, and serial data.
Baud rate
The speed of a serial connection. Both ends must agree exactly, or you get garbage/zeros.
LiPo / 18650
Rechargeable lithium batteries. LiPo is a flat light pouch; 18650 is a AA-sized cylinder with more capacity.
HNSW
The fast "nearest-neighbour" search the Seed uses to recognise a fingerprint instantly among thousands of stored ones.
